United States Senate
Position Paper of Senator Chuck Schumer (D-NY)
Topic: Addressing the War Powers

As Senate Majority Leader, I, Senator Chuck Schumer, representing the people of the great state of New York, recognize the separation of powers as a core founding principle that our constitutional fathers intended. Article I vests the powers to declare war firmly in this Congress, to raise a standing army, and to take command over the state militia. Our founding fathers intended to establish a system of checks and balances to prevent unilateral executive action without legislative authorization.

However, modern conflicts often begin as imperfect engagements 4 U.S. (4 Dall.) 37 (1800) that do not fit the traditional definition of “hostilities” as our founding fathers had in mind. The War Powers Resolution (WPR) gave Congress a check over presidents who have repeatedly and continuously used the “unitary executive theory” to expand the Executive Branche’s power including executive privilege 542 U.S. 367 (2004), with one simple goal: to clarify and restrain the President’s ability to commit forces without congressional approval. This goal has never been fully achieved; when litigated by members of this House or the people we represent, the case often becomes moot or is dismissed by federal courts.

Recently, President Donald John Trump has ordered military actions, such as bombing Iran, without prior consultation with Congress, as well as mobilizing the California National Guard in response to the so-called invasion. Nesessitating limits of presidential power in military actions without Congressional authorization 343 U.S. 579 (1952) We all agree that wars are imperfect, but this issue extends beyond party lines. When one executive reaches too far for power, their successors will look to the precedent that we set today. This Senator moves to draw parallels and vest powers in the Select Committees of both Houses, requiring consultation and approval in appropriate cases to use military force, with checks from members of our Congress.

The WPR was designed to prevent wars from escalating unchecked, yet we lack a formal system to terminate conflicts. The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) has been broadly applied beyond its original intent based on the sitting president's discretion. Furthermore, the WPR struggles to address modern warfare tactics, 2019 Yemen War Powers Resolution saw a presidental veto, barring this congress from checking the president's power without political checks.

Therefore, I advocate for reforms such as requiring explicit congressional approval for all military actions except immediate self-defense, establishing joint congressional committees to authorize operations, and enforcing the 60-day limit on deployments more rigorously. These proposals aim to restore constitutional balance and prevent executive overreach.

In conclusion, my position is one of measured leadership: to ensure that the War Powers Resolution remains a vital tool for congressional oversight, safeguarding our constitutional balance, and promoting responsible U.S. foreign policy. I stand committed to working with all Senators—across party lines—to uphold these principles and protect the American people from unnecessary military entanglements while preserving our nation’s security interests. Bipartisan cooperation is essential to address these challenges effectively and to reaffirm Congress’s rightful role in decisions of war and peace.


Chuck Schumer
